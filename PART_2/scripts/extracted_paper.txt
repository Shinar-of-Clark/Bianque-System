ABSTRACT The increasing failure rate of underground cable systems due to aging poses challenges for the
reliable operation of power grids. Without proper asset management methods, the grid may not adequately
support the ongoing climate-centered electrification of society. This highlights the need for strategic asset
maintenance decision-making. While the existing literature includes inventive approaches for reliability
modeling and maintenance strategy selection, there is still a gap in integrating detailed predictive analytics-
based reliability modeling that accounts for common data deficiencies, alongside maintenance strategies that
consider both reliability and cost-based performance indicators. In this paper, we present a comprehensive
proactive cable replacement method using the Neural Weibull Proportional Hazard model, specifically
designed to handle truncation and censoring mechanisms. The method employs a Bayesian nested Monte
Carlo simulation to address the dual uncertainties associated with the probabilistic reliability model. We
apply this maintenance simulation method to a case study of a real distribution system in Denmark. Our
results indicate that the optimal replacement strategy reduces outage costs by up to 6.16 million DKK and can
decrease the System Average Interruption Duration Index (SAIDI) by more than 50% compared to alternative
replacement strategies. Finally, a sensitivity analysis of the maintenance budget shows that operators can
reduce their annual budget for cable replacements by up to 50%, incurring only minor additional costs
and an increase in peak SAIDI of just 1.5 minutes. The innovation lies in combining the NWPH model
with Bayesian Monte Carlo simulation to handle incomplete records, quantify uncertainty, and guide cost-
effective maintenance decisions.

ies are often experimental, and the methods typically do not
differentiate between individual cables. Instead, prior studies
typically assess failure risk at aggregated units entire feeders
or network areas rather than at the individual-cable level,

Other studies focus directly on optimizing maintenance

However, these methodologies commonly lack predictive
analytics for time-dependent failure rates and asset-specific
features. While [10] employed multi-objective optimization
via the epsilon-constraint method considering cost-based
metrics, it did not address time-dependent failure rates.
More comprehensive frameworks have explicitly incorpo-
rated time-dependent failure rates. For example, [11] pre-
sented a multi-period cable replacement planning method
optimizing reliability indices using MILP and considering
network expansions. However, detailed operational and envi-
ronmental conditions or intrinsic asset features beyond cable
length were overlooked.
Other approaches balance operational and capital expen-
ditures through innovative modeling. [12] utilized system
dynamics optimized via a Weibull generalized process that
incorporates imperfect maintenance scenarios. Similarly, [13]
proposed a techno-economic framework integrating instal-
lation and outage costs using a failure model combining
Markov chains and failure distributions. Y et, both studies
neglect operational and environmental conditions, intrinsic
asset characteristics, and data limitations such as truncation
and censoring.
Several other studies have aimed for more detailed fail-
ure modeling by correlating operational and environmental
conditions with failure rates. For instance, [14] proposed a
remaining useful life (RUL) estimation method for under-
ground cables, using operational conditions and intrinsic asset
features to determine an overall health index combined with
statistical reliability modeling. Although effective in mod-
eling aging trends, the approach did not account for failure
consequences in maintenance interventions. Conversely, [15]
combined a Weibull Proportional Hazard (WPH) model with
an importance metric reflecting failure consequences to create
a cable risk map, advising caution based on cable locations.
Similarly, [16] used fuzzy risk assessment correlating geospa-
tial data and Partial discharge data. However, these methods
lack comprehensive consideration of data deficiencies and
validation of maintenance impacts.
Additional studies applied proportional hazard models and
inventive reliability approaches. In [17], a proportional haz-
ard model analyzed feature correlations with low voltage
cable failures, providing insights but lacking failure conse-
quence consideration and truncation accommodation. [18]
and [19] integrated the Arrhenius relation with Weibull distri-
butions; [18] studied reliability indices and thermal life loss,
while [19] optimized replacement planning, incorporating
distributed energy resources and network reconfigurations.
However, both studies manually set Weibull parameters with-
out utilizing actual failure data.
Complementary Bayesian approaches in infrastructure also
inform our setting: under extreme events, enhanced Hierar-
chical Sparse Bayesian Learning has been used to interpret
and forecast heteroscedastic SHM measurements with quan-
tified uncertainty, illustrating robustness to volatile sensing
conditions [20]. Likewise, Bayesian networks provide a flexi-
ble, updateable framework for system-level reliability assess-
ment in complex structures [21]. These works contextualize
and support our Bayesian treatment of uncertainty adopted in
this study.
While many inventive approaches have been proposed,
there remains a gap in the integrated consideration of pre-
dictive analytics for data-driven reliability modeling, cost
metrics, and reliability indices in strategic cable maintenance
and replacement planning. Moreover, the current literature
often overlooks the importance of addressing common data
deficiencies such as censoring and truncation.
To address these limitations, we propose a maintenance
strategy selection method based on a Neural Weibull Pro-
portional Hazard (NWPH) model, which incorporates op-
erational and environmental conditions along with intrinsic
cable features. The model’s likelihood function accommo-
dates both truncation and censoring mechanisms. To handle
the probabilistic nature of the NWPH model, the strategy
selection process employs a Bayesian nested Monte Carlo
simulation, using Bayesian bootstrap samples to represent
parameter uncertainty.
Beyond feeder-level ranking and MILP-based renewal
planning, recent studies are moving toward deep survival or
related time-to-failure learning. A temporal-CNN survival
model predicts failure-time distributions for urban infras-
tructure (including an electricity dataset), but it assumes
rich sensing and predominantly right-censored data, not the
left truncation and interval censoring common in utility
records [22]. For grid equipment, a Bayesian deep-learning
LSTM estimates transformer failure rates with uncertainty
bands from condition data, yet it does not handle left trun-
cation/interval censoring or connect predictions to plan-
ning KPIs [23]. For transmission lines, a CNN-LSTM with
TimeGAN targets remaining useful life rather than calibrated
survival curves and likewise omits truncation/censoring treat-
ment [24]. In contrast, our NWPH is designed for cable-
level decisions: it maximizes the full likelihood with ex-
plicit left truncation and right/interval censoring, learns non-
linear asset-environment effects, and retains closed-form sur-
vival/quantiles for SAIDI/SAIFI and outage-cost planning.

II. METHODOLOGY
This section presents the proposed method for proactive cable
replacement planning. We first introduce the NWPH model,
which estimates failure probabilities while explicitly handling
data deficiencies such as truncation and censoring. We then
describe how the model’s hazard and reliability functions
are used to rank cables by their fault vulnerability. Finally,
we outline the maintenance simulation framework, which
integrates these reliability estimates into a Bayesian nested
Monte Carlo procedure for evaluating maintenance strategies
under uncertainty.

failure risk to be captured. The failure density, hazard rate,

1) Data Deficiencies
This section addresses the challenges associated with in-
complete failure data, which are common in power distri-
bution systems. Specifically, it outlines how data censoring
and truncation affect reliability modeling and how they are
incorporated into the likelihood function.
Estimating the parameters of a reliability model is typically
carried out using maximum likelihood estimation, which be-
comes straightforward when the failure time, T , is known for
all observations. In such cases, the likelihood function is given
by [28]:
L(θ|t) = Pr(T = t|θ) = f (t, θ) (4)
where θ denotes the set of model parameters, i.e., θ = λ, γ, ω,
with ω representing the parameters of the neural network.
We fit θ by maximizing the full log-likelihood in Eq. (4),
which includes uncensored, right-censored, and interval-
censored terms and corrects left truncation by dividing each
contribution by R(y) (the reliability at the observation start).
Inputs are Min–Max scaled to [0, 1], and gradients are
clipped at 0.1 for stability. We use AdamW (lr 10−3, β =
(0.9, 0.999)), initialize (λ, γ) via an iterative 50 × 50 grid
search over [10−3, 1] × [1, 9] with g(z) ≡ 0 (Algorithm 1),
and clamp λ ≥ 10−3, γ ≥ 1.1 during training. Early stopping
evaluates the training loss every 50 epochs (patience 250; max
5000).
However, in practice, failure times for many assets are
not observed, most commonly because the asset has not yet
failed during the observation period. When the exact failure
time is not known, the data is said to be censored. Four
types of censoring are typically distinguished (including the
uncensored case), as outlined below:
1) Uncensored: The failure time is observed exactly and
is known to be equal to some time T = t.
2) Left-censored: The failure time is only known to have
occurred before a certain time, i.e., T < t.
3) Right-censored: The failure time is only known to
have occurred after a certain time, i.e., T > t.
4) Interval-censored: The failure time is known to lie
between two time points, tl < T < tr , where tl and
tr denote the lower and upper bounds of the interval
within which the failure occurred.
In addition to censoring mechanisms, an important source
of bias in reliability and survival models is truncation,
with left-truncation being particularly relevant [29]. Left-
truncation occurs when assets are not observed from the
time they become at risk, which typically corresponds to
their installation. This situation often arises in systems where
maintenance records have not been maintained since the ini-
tial deployment of assets. As a result, early failures are not
captured, and assets that are older or associated with a higher
relative risk are underrepresented in the dataset. These assets
are also more likely to fail, making their absence from the
data especially problematic.
2) Parameter Estimation under Censoring and Truncation
When failure times are not fully observed, the likelihood
function must be adjusted to reflect the type of censoring or
truncation present in the data. For left-censored observations,
the likelihood is defined as [28]:
L(θ | t) = Pr(T < t | θ) = 1 − R(t | θ) (5)
For right-censored observations, the likelihood is expressed
as
L(θ | t) = Pr(T > t | θ) = R(t | θ) (6)
For interval-censored observations, the likelihood is given by
L(θ | t) = Pr(tl < T < tr | θ) = R(tl | θ) − R(tr | θ) (7)
Left-truncation is accounted for by applying inverse reliabil-
ity weighting, where each likelihood contribution is divided
by R(y), with y denoting the age at which observation of the
asset began [28].

function is defined as

3) Optimizing the Log-Likelihood Loss Function
To train the NWPH model effectively, a loss function based
on the log-likelihood is used, which measures how well the
model explains the observed failure data, even when some
events are only partially known due to censoring or trun-
cation. To maximize the likelihood, a logarithmic transfor-
mation is first applied. The resulting log-likelihood function
serves as the basis for training the parameters of the NWPH
model using gradient-based optimization. In this process,
parameters are iteratively updated based on the partial deriva-
tives of the loss with respect to each parameter, aiming to
minimize the negative log-likelihood.
However, as the likelihood approaches zero, the gradients
can become extremely large, which may destabilize training.
To mitigate this issue, a scaled version of the loss function is
employed, similar to the approach adopted in [30]:
LL∗(θ | t) = 1
N ln(L(θ | t)) (9)
where N denotes the batch size. Since large-magnitude co-
variates can cause the likelihood to approach zero at initializa-
tion, covariates are scaled to the range [0, 1] prior to training.
Despite this, the loss remains sensitive to the parameterization
of the model.
It was observed that, on occasion, a gradient update may
lead to a region in the parameter space where the loss becomes
numerically unstable or diverges to infinity. To address this
and improve training robustness, gradient clipping is applied.
This technique limits the magnitude of the gradients, thereby
preventing abrupt changes in parameter values and ensuring
stable convergence.
4) Hazard Initialization
Although loss scaling and gradient clipping improve the ro-
bustness of the training process, they often lead to slower
convergence. To address this issue, an initialization heuristic
is introduced, as outlined in Algorithm 1. This heuristic per-
forms an iterative, grid-based search to find suitable initial
values for the Weibull scale (λ) and shape (γ) parameters that
best align with the observed data.

2) Cause-Based Fault Features
Given the limited observability in medium-voltage power
distribution systems, the dataset is not constructed to empha-
size symptom-based fault features such as partial discharges
resulting from insulation degradation. Instead, the focus is on
features related to the initial causes of cable deterioration.
These cause-based features serve as proxies for the under-
lying degradation mechanisms and fall into three main cat-
egories: environmental, loading-related, and asset-specific.
Notably, environmental and asset features require less so-
phisticated internal data collection from utility companies,
making them more practical for widespread implementation.
a: Environmental Features
Environmental features describe the external conditions sur-
rounding a cable, such as soil type, moisture level, proximity
to roads, and nearby excavation activity. These factors are
known to influence underground cable failure rates. While
detailed physical degradation models are unavailable, statisti-
cal and machine learning approaches can capture correlations
between such environmental conditions and failure likeli-
hood. To convert environmental data into usable features, the
geometric nature of each data type is considered.
b: Asset Features
Asset features include properties such as conductor material,
insulation material, and joint types, which are extracted di-
rectly from the asset database with minimal preprocessing.
Among these, joint information requires some processing,
specifically, the number of joints of each type connected to
a cable’s endpoints is computed and used as input features.
C. MAINTENANCE STRATEGY SIMULATION
This section presents a simulation framework for evaluating
maintenance strategies based on the NWPH model in the con-
text of cable renewal. A maintenance strategy is defined by its
reactive and proactive interventions. Reactive interventions
occur after a failure and determine whether the failed cable
is fully replaced or only repaired i.e., replacing the damaged
segment while keeping the rest in service. Proactive interven-
tions are executed before failure and involve the preventive
replacement of cables.
Accordingly, we define a reactive strategy governed by a
threshold ωNOPF for the maximum allowed number of past
failures. Additionally, a cable is replaced if its remaining
length after repair falls below ωlength, representing a practical
limit for keeping short cable segments in operation.
The proactive strategy determines which cables should be
replaced in advance. A central aspect of this strategy is the
ranking metric used to prioritize replacements. The mainte-
nance planner considers the following ranking metrics:
1) Naïve: Random cable ranking.
2) Vulnerability: Cables ranked by hazard rate divided by
replacement cost.
3) Consequence: Cables ranked by number of interrupted
consumers per unit cost.

III. CASE STUDY
The proposed methodology is applied to a Danish 10 kV
power distribution network, illustrated in Figure 4. The main-
tenance and outage records used in this case study were
provided by the Danish distribution system operator under
a data-use agreement. Under this agreement, the underly-
ing records cannot be publicly shared or redistributed. From
2016 to 2022, the grid experienced over 90 cable-related
interruptions, as documented in the operator’s maintenance
records. Only failures attributed to aging or material degrada-
tion were included, while those caused by excavation or other
unpredictable factors were excluded. The network comprises
a mix of cable types: PEX cables, primarily installed from
the 1990s onward, and older paper-insulated lead-covered
(PILC/APB) cables, installed before 1990. The grid operator,
is actively replacing APB cables, which exhibit significantly
higher failure rates due to their advanced age and shorter
expected service life. A subset of the APB cables lacks
recorded installation dates. For failed cables with missing in-
stallation dates, the failure age is treated as interval-censored.
Furthermore, because installation records predate 1990 and
the maintenance data only begin in 2016, the dataset is sub-
ject to left-truncation. Overall, the dataset includes primarily
right-censored observations, along with 56 uncensored and
37 interval-censored cases. Cables for which the pre-failure
condition could not be reliably reconstructed were excluded
from the analysis.

the least expensive. The average unplanned outage duration is
approximately 45 minutes. Using the NWPH model and sim-
ulation framework, we assess the effectiveness of proactive
maintenance strategies and examine how varying the replace-
ment budget impacts the potential to delay investments in

A. PERFORMANCE OF THE NWPH MODEL
Loading features were excluded from the dataset, as they did
not improve the NWPH model’s performance, as shown in
Figure 5. The figure compares three approaches: the proposed
NWPH model, a naïve (random) ranking, and the Weibull
Length Proportional (WBLP) model. The WBLP model is a
simplified version of the Weibull Proportional Hazards model
that assumes the hazard rate depends only on cable length.
Model performance is evaluated using repeated stratified
cross-validation, which ensures that each data split maintains
a balanced proportion of censored, uncensored, and interval-
censored cases. The figure shows the average FCLC curves
for the three methods. This curve illustrates how well each
method identifies high-risk cables. For example, a point at
(0.2, 0.25) means that the top 20% of the network length
(ranked by predicted risk) contains 25% of all failures.
It is surprising that the WBLP model performs worse than a
random ranking. Normally, we expect older and longer cables
to fail more often, but the results do not support this. There
are a few possible reasons. First, many cables have unknown
installation dates, and their actual ages might be higher than
others. Second, some physical factors might affect failure risk
in ways that age and length alone cannot explain. Finally, the
data may not be very accurate. Since most cable ages were
estimated from old financial records, there is a chance of
human error.

B. EFFECTS OF TRUNCATION AND CENSORING
Figure 6 compares reliability predictions from three NWPH
model variants:
• A full model that accounts for truncation, interval-
censoring, and right-censoring (red),
• A model that ignores truncation but includes interval-
censoring and right-censoring (blue),
• A model that only accounts for right-censoring (purple).
Uncensored data is included in all cases. The results show
that ignoring interval-censoring slightly overestimates relia-
bility. The most significant effect, however, is from trunca-
tion. When truncation is not handled, the model assumes no
failures happen before cable age 30. This creates an unre-
alistic delay in the reliability decline, because early failures
are missing from the data. These missing failures are due to
incomplete maintenance records rather than an actual absence
of failures.
When truncation is accounted for, the model begins the
decline in reliability earlier and more gradually. This pattern
is more realistic and provides more accurate predictions.

G. COMPUTATIONAL CONSIDERATIONS AND SCALING
Our nested design runs B = 100 bootstrap fits and K = 100
inner Monte-Carlo simulations per strategy. For a network
with N cables over a T -year horizon, one full evaluation
scales roughly with B × K × S ×(N log N + NT ): the N log N
term comes from ranking assets by risk, and NT from yearly
simulation of failures and replacements. The outer bootstrap
models and inner simulations are independent, so evaluations
parallelize naturally across CPUs/GPUs. For larger grids,
the same methodology can be adopted with practical speed-
ups: (i) adaptive inner simulation (increase K only until
SAIDI/SAIFI/cost standard errors fall below a target) and
(ii) lightweight surrogates to interpolate KPI-versus-budget
curves from a few budget points. These options preserve
conclusions while reducing runtime and compute cost.

V. CONCLUSION
This study introduced the NWPH model combined with a
Bayesian nested Monte Carlo simulation framework to eval-
uate maintenance strategies for the replacement of PILC
(APB) cables in electrical distribution networks. The NWPH
model effectively addresses common data limitations such
as censoring and truncation, which are often neglected in
engineering reliability modeling. Our findings demonstrated
the significant impact these data issues have on reliability
predictions and underscored the value of properly accounting
for them.
The NWPH model produced informative and robust relia-
bility estimates, with low predictive uncertainty reflected by
a maximum interquartile range of 0.12. Compared to conven-
tional ranking approaches based solely on cable age or length,
the NWPH-based strategy provided superior performance in
identifying vulnerable assets.
Simulation results confirmed that the risk-based mainte-
nance strategy was the most cost-effective. It reduced outage
costs by up to 6.16 million DKK and SAIDI by as much as
4 minutes, compared to alternative strategies. Additionally,
sensitivity analysis revealed that the annual maintenance bud-
get could be halved with only a modest increase in SAIDI
(1.5 minutes) and cumulative outage costs (10 million DKK).
These results suggest that network operators can allocate
resources more efficiently, prioritizing critical upgrades like
grid reinforcements without compromising service reliability.
Overall, the proposed framework offers a practical and
scalable decision-support tool for asset management, en-
abling data-informed, cost-conscious, and risk-aware main-
tenance planning.
